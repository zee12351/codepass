## DAY 1 — QOTD: Modifier 59
🧠 CPC Question of the Day

A physician performs two distinct procedures in the same session, at separate anatomical sites that are normally bundled together under NCCI edits. Which modifier signals they should be paid separately?

A) 25
B) 51
C) 59
D) 76

Your answer 👇

**(Answer comment, post later):** C — **Modifier 59 (Distinct Procedural Service)**. It unbundles procedures that NCCI edits would normally combine, when they're truly separate sites/sessions. Modifier 51 just flags "multiple procedures" — it does NOT break a bundle. Mixing up 59 and 51 is a classic exam trap.

## DAY 2 — Value: 3 mistakes that quietly fail people on the CPC
Most people don't fail the CPC because they don't know coding. They fail for 3 avoidable reasons:

1) **Pacing.** ~2 minutes per question. Spend 6 on a tough one and you'll run out of time with easy points left on the table.

2) **Reaching for "unspecified."** If the documentation gives you laterality or specificity, an unspecified code is a wrong answer — and a real-world denial.

3) **Modifier 25 vs 59.** 25 = a separate, significant E/M on the same day as a procedure. 59 = a distinct procedural service. Knowing which is which is free points.

Fix these three and your score jumps before you learn a single new code.

## DAY 3 — QOTD: ICD-10 combination codes
🧠 CPC Question of the Day

Documentation: "Type 2 diabetes mellitus with diabetic polyneuropathy." How do you report it?

A) One combination code (e.g., E11.42)
B) Polyneuropathy first, then diabetes
C) Two unrelated codes, any order
D) Diabetes unspecified only

Your answer 👇

**(Answer comment):** A — ICD-10-CM uses **combination codes** for diabetes with many manifestations, so E11.42 captures *both* the diabetes and the neuropathy in a single code. No need for two codes. Knowing when a combination code exists saves time and prevents sequencing errors.

## DAY 5 — QOTD: New vs Established patient
🧠 CPC Question of the Day

A patient last seen by the same physician (same specialty, same group) **2 years ago** returns today. New or established patient?

A) New patient
B) Established patient

Your answer 👇

**(Answer comment):** B — **Established.** A patient is only "new" if they haven't received a face-to-face professional service from that physician (or another of the same specialty in the group) in the **past 3 years**. 2 years = established. The "3-year rule" shows up on the exam constantly.

## DAY 6 — Myth-buster
Myth: "To pass the CPC you have to memorize thousands of codes."

Truth: the CPC is **open book**. You bring your codebooks. The exam isn't testing memory — it's testing how **fast and accurately you navigate** the books and apply the guidelines under time pressure.

That's good news and bad news:
✅ You don't need a photographic memory.
⚠️ But speed and book-navigation are skills you have to *practice* — you can't cram them the night before.

The people who pass aren't the ones who memorized the most. They're the ones who practiced the most reps.

## DAY 7 — QOTD: Professional vs Technical component
🧠 CPC Question of the Day

A radiologist only **interprets** an X-ray taken at a hospital (reviews the images, writes the report) — they don't own the equipment. Which modifier reports just their professional work?

A) TC (technical component)
B) 26 (professional component)
C) 59
D) 51

Your answer 👇

**(Answer comment):** B — **Modifier 26 (professional component)** covers the physician's interpretation and report. **TC** covers the equipment/technician side. When one entity owns the machine and another reads the result, the service is split — 26 for the read, TC for the tech. A favorite radiology-coding exam scenario.
